Plan your route, check the weather forecast ahead of time, carry the right gear and always plan for the worse.

HYPOTHERMIA

Hypothermia is a medical emergency that occurs when your body loses heat faster than it can produce heat, causing a dangerously low body temperature. It occurs when your body loses heat faster than it produces it. The most common causes of hypothermia are exposure to cold-weather conditions or cold water. But prolonged exposure to any environment colder than your body can lead to hypothermia if you aren't dressed appropriately or can't control the conditions.

AVALANCHES

An avalanche is a mass of snow that slides rapidly down an inclined slope, such as a mountainside or the roof of a building. Avalanches are triggered by either natural forces (e.g. precipitation, wind drifting snow, rapid temperature changes) or human activity. In mountainous terrain, they are among the most serious hazards to human life and property. Avalanches are sometimes called snowslides. An avalanche occurs when stress from the pull of gravity and/or applied load (such as a skier) exceeds the strength of the snow cover.

HOW TO PROTECT YOURSELF
The most important actions you can take to survive an avalanche are done before it happens.
GET TRAINING
Get training on how to recognize hazardous conditions and avalanche - prone locations.
GET EDUCATED
Learn how to properly use safety equipment.
GET ALERTS
Sign up for alerts on current avalanche dangers.
PROPER EQUIPMENT
Get proper equipment to protect yourself from head injuries and create air pockets.
USE DEVICES
Use devices to support rescue.
HAVE COMPANION
Always have a buddy, preferably one familiar with the area.
COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID
Common mistakes can become deadly. Constant vigilance can help avoid some of these common errors.
DON’T STOP
Don’t stop in an exposed area on a slope—your risk of getting caught in a fast-moving avalanche increases.
DON’T SEPARATE
Don’t get too separated from your group members so that you can’t hear or see each other.
AVOID BEING CLOSE
Avoid travelling immediately above a partner; this risks triggering an avalanche on the person below.
NO DISTRACTIONS
Don’t get too distracted by taking photos or videos that you lose sight of avalanche risks.
ROUTES TO AVOID
Avoiding these slopes / routes can significantly impact your overall safety.
  1. NORTH-FACING ASPECTS

In winter, generally opt for south-facing slopes. Because the sun melts and condenses snow, they’re more stable. North-facing slopes tend to be more powdery, but often have unstable layers.

  1. STEEP SLOPES

Large slides typically start on slopes between 30 and 45 degrees. Shallower angles usually don’t initiate slides, and sheer steeps don’t hold much snow, so huge movement is less likely.

  1. BOWLS AND GULLIES

Steep, open slopes and bowls, as well as ravines, are likely avalanche zones. Safer areas include ridge crests, thick forests (D), wide valleys, and windward slopes (that don’t have big snow deposits hanging above).

  1. CRACKING SNOW

Be on alert if you see cracks in the surface, you hear a whumph sound, the surface feels hollow, or you see snowballs rolling downhill. These are all avy indicators.

  1. PREVIOUS PATHS

If there’s old slide evidence—like a big, open chute through a forest, broken trees (E), or a debris pile, avoid the trap and move quickly out of the danger zone.
